\begin{abstract}
Pretrained vision models encode object similarity differently from humans, and
prior work shows that a learned linear transform of frozen features closes much of
this gap. We focus on a question that line of work leaves open: \emph{is the
required correction shared across architectures, or particular to each model?}
Using the THINGS odd-one-out benchmark (4.70M human triplet judgments over 1{,}854
objects), we learn a single L2-regularized linear transform per model for five
frozen backbones spanning self-supervised (DINOv2), contrastive (CLIP, SigLIP),
and supervised (ViT, ResNet-50) training, and ask how those transforms
\emph{transfer} across architectures. Two results reproduce and consolidate prior
findings across a broad zoo: a cheap linear map lifts \emph{every} backbone to
88--92\% of the human noise ceiling (0.59--0.62 odd-one-out accuracy), erasing
large initial differences between training paradigms (largest gain for the weakest
model, DINOv2 $+0.20$; Table~\ref{tab:main}: mean$\pm$std over three seeds); and
the same abstract/functional categories (office
supplies, furniture, musical instruments, medical equipment) resist linear
alignment across all backbones. Our central contribution is a \emph{controlled}
cross-model transfer analysis with a result we did not expect. Naively, a transform
learned on one model recovers only $\sim$19\% of another model's within-model
alignment gain---suggesting the correction is model-specific. But transferring in an
independently-reduced PCA space conflates representational difference with arbitrary
basis mismatch: after an orthogonal-Procrustes alignment that rigidly rotates each
model into a common coordinate frame (fit on the shared concept correspondence, no
human labels, and distortion-free by construction), recovery jumps to $88\%$
($84$--$92\%$). The apparent model-specificity is thus almost entirely a PCA-basis
artifact; the human-alignment correction is largely \emph{shared} across
self-supervised, contrastive, and supervised backbones, leaving only a small
($\sim$12\%) architecture-specific residual. Representational similarity to the human
SPoSE embedding mirrors the alignment result (Spearman $0.14$--$0.42 \to
0.78$--$0.83$), and an image-disjoint analysis bounds image-level leakage at 1--6
points, smallest for contrastive models.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Whether the internal representations of vision models agree with human perception
has become a central question for robustness, interpretability, and transfer
\citep{sucholutsky2023alignment,muttenthaler2024aligning}. Models that better
match human similarity structure tend to generalize and resist distribution shift
more gracefully \citep{muttenthaler2024aligning}, yet off-the-shelf backbones
still diverge substantially from human judgments. A practical question follows:
\emph{can this divergence be cheaply corrected, and is the correction universal?}

We study the simplest possible correction---a single linear transform of
\emph{frozen} embeddings---against large-scale human behavior. The THINGS
initiative \citep{hebart2019things,hebart2023things} provides 4.70M odd-one-out
judgments over 1{,}854 everyday objects, operationalizing human object similarity
at a scale that supports reliable training and evaluation. Given frozen features
$x$ from a pretrained model, we ask: how far can a learned linear map $W$ push
$Wx$ toward agreement with human odd-one-out choices, and does a $W$ learned on
one architecture transfer to another? The transfer question is diagnostic: strong
transfer would imply the misalignment is a shared linear distortion of a common,
nearly human-like space; weak transfer would imply deeper, model-specific
structure that no single linear patch can fix. We find the answer turns on a
methodological control: naive transfer in independently-reduced spaces suggests
model-specific structure, but a distortion-free basis alignment reverses that
verdict---the correction is largely shared.

We evaluate five backbones that deliberately span training objectives---DINOv2
(self-supervised), CLIP and SigLIP (image--text contrastive), and ViT and
ResNet-50 (ImageNet-supervised)---so that conclusions are not specific to one
training recipe. Our pipeline is light: features are extracted once from frozen
models, and only a low-parameter linear map is trained, making the entire study
reproducible on a single commodity GPU from public data.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{A controlled cross-model transfer analysis} (our main contribution):
holding the alignment recipe fixed across a five-model zoo, we show that a transform
learned on one architecture recovers $\sim$88\% of another's alignment gain
\emph{once} the models are placed in a common coordinate frame by a rigid
orthogonal-Procrustes alignment. Naive transfer in independently-reduced PCA spaces
recovers only $\sim$19\%, so the apparent model-specificity is almost entirely a
basis artifact---the human-alignment correction is largely \emph{shared} across
architectures. To our knowledge this is the first cross-model transfer analysis of
human-alignment transforms at this breadth on THINGS, and it shows the
basis-alignment control is essential to reading the result correctly.
\item \textbf{A consolidation of two known results across a broad zoo}: a cheap
linear transform brings \emph{all} backbones to 88--92\% of the human ceiling with
the largest gains for the weakest models, and a shared set of abstract/functional
categories resists alignment everywhere. These \emph{corroborate}
\citet{muttenthaler2023human,muttenthaler2024aligning} rather than claim novelty; we
report them because they hold uniformly across our five-model span and motivate the
transfer question.
\item \textbf{An image-disjoint robustness analysis} isolating a small (1--6 point)
image-leakage component that is smallest for contrastive models, plus a fully
scripted, reproducible benchmark (download, feature extraction, alignment,
transfer, analysis, robustness) from public data.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related work}
\paragraph{Human alignment of representations.}
\citet{muttenthaler2023human} show that linear ``transform'' probes optimized
against human similarity improve correspondence to behavior, that architecture has
little effect on the alignment achievable this way, and that abstract concepts
resist alignment while concrete ones do not; \citet{muttenthaler2024aligning} extend
this across abstraction levels and link alignment to downstream robustness. Our
near-ceiling alignability (Table~\ref{tab:main}) and shared-semantic-ceiling
(Table~\ref{tab:cats}) results \emph{directly corroborate} these reports and are not
claimed as novel. Our distinct contribution is to hold the alignment recipe fixed
and \emph{contrast architectures}---asking whether the learned correction is
\emph{shared}---and to answer it, largely in the affirmative, with a cross-model
transfer experiment plus an orthogonal-Procrustes basis-alignment control that prior
work does not perform.
\citet{sucholutsky2023alignment} survey the broader notion of representational
alignment and its measurement.

\paragraph{The THINGS similarity structure.}
The THINGS database \citep{hebart2019things} and its CC0 image extension
\citep{stoinski2024thingsplus} provide the stimuli, while
\citet{hebart2020revealing} derive a sparse, interpretable embedding (SPoSE) of
human object similarity from triplet judgments, later scaled in
\citet{hebart2023things}. We use the odd-one-out triplets as the supervision and
evaluation signal, and the SPoSE embedding as a reference for representational
similarity analysis.

\paragraph{Comparing model and human representations.}
Representational similarity analysis \citep{kriegeskorte2008rsa} compares systems
through their pairwise (dis)similarity matrices and is widely used to relate model
and brain/behavior. Odd-one-out accuracy offers a complementary, decision-level
metric that avoids arbitrary thresholds. We report both, and additionally study
the \emph{transfer} of alignment transforms across architectures---with a
basis-alignment control so the comparison is not an artifact of independently-fit
shared spaces---which to our knowledge is underexplored at this breadth on THINGS.

\section{Method}\label{sec:method}

\paragraph{Data.} We use the 1{,}854 THINGS reference objects with one
copyright-free (CC0) image per concept \citep{stoinski2024thingsplus}, and the
THINGS odd-one-out triplets \citep{hebart2023things}: 4.12M training, 0.45M
validation, and a 15.6k held-out noise-ceiling test set (the repeatedly-sampled
high-agreement triplets used to estimate human consistency). Triplets are
0-indexed and stored with the chosen-similar pair first and the odd-one-out last.
The empirical human noise ceiling is $0.673$ and chance is $1/3$.

\paragraph{Backbones.} Five frozen models spanning training objectives: DINOv2
ViT-B/14 (self-supervised), CLIP ViT-B/16 and SigLIP ViT-B/16 (image--text
contrastive), and ViT-B/16 and ResNet-50 (ImageNet-supervised)
\citep{oquab2024dinov2,radford2021clip,zhai2023siglip,dosovitskiy2021vit,he2016resnet}.
For each image we extract a single embedding (pooled/CLS feature), yielding a
matrix $X\in\mathbb{R}^{1854\times d}$ per model with $d\in\{512,768,2048\}$.

\paragraph{Odd-one-out decision rule.} For a triplet $(a,b,o)$ with human-chosen
similar pair $(a,b)$ and odd-one-out $o$, we compute cosine similarities between
embeddings and predict as the odd item the one least similar to the other two,
equivalently choosing the most similar pair:
\begin{equation}
\hat{o} = \arg\min_{i\in\{a,b,o\}}\ \textstyle\sum_{j\neq i}\cos(z_i,z_j),
\qquad
\text{correct} \iff \cos(z_a,z_b) \ge \max\{\cos(z_a,z_o),\cos(z_b,z_o)\}.
\end{equation}
Accuracy is the fraction of triplets for which the predicted odd item matches the
human choice.

\paragraph{Linear alignment.} We standardize each feature dimension
($\hat{x} = (x-\mu)/\sigma$ using training statistics) and learn a transform
$z = W\hat{x}$, $W\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times d}$. Following the SPoSE/VICE
formulation, we treat the triplet as a 3-way classification over candidate
similar pairs. With $\bar z = z/\lVert z\rVert$ and a learnable temperature
$\tau$, the logits and loss for a triplet are
\begin{equation}
\ell = \tau \big[\cos(\bar z_a,\bar z_b),\ \cos(\bar z_a,\bar z_o),\ \cos(\bar z_b,\bar z_o)\big],
\qquad
\mathcal{L} = \mathrm{CE}\!\left(\ell,\, y{=}0\right) + \lambda\lVert W\rVert_F^2,
\end{equation}
where target $y{=}0$ is the human-chosen pair $(a,b)$. We initialize $W$ at the
identity (so training starts from the cosine zero-shot solution), optimize with
Adam, and early-stop on validation accuracy. Cosine similarity with a learnable
temperature keeps the softmax well-conditioned; using L2-normalized embeddings
makes the aligned metric consistent with the zero-shot baseline.

\paragraph{Cross-model transfer.} To compare transforms across differing
dimensionalities, we standardize and PCA-reduce every model to a shared 256-d
space, learn $W$ per model there, and measure odd-one-out accuracy when applying a
source transform $W_{\text{src}}$ to a target model's features
$\hat z_{\text{tgt}}$. We summarize transfer by the \emph{recovery fraction},
the share of a target's own-model alignment gain (over the $W{=}I$ baseline)
captured by the average off-diagonal (transferred) transform:
$\rho_t = (\bar a^{\text{off}}_t - a^{I}_t)/(a^{\text{own}}_t - a^{I}_t)$.

\paragraph{Basis-alignment control.}\label{par:procrustes} The shared space is
obtained by reducing each model \emph{independently}, so two models' principal axes
need not lie in a common coordinate frame; a transform $W_{\text{src}}$ applied to
$\hat z_{\text{tgt}}$ is then penalized for basis mismatch on top of any genuinely
model-specific structure, which could inflate apparent specificity. Because every
model embeds the \emph{same} 1{,}854 concepts in the same row order, we can remove
this confound with an orthogonal-Procrustes rotation: for each (source, target) pair
we solve
\begin{equation}
R^\star = \arg\min_{R^\top R = I}\ \big\lVert \hat Z_{\text{tgt}} R - \hat Z_{\text{src}} \big\rVert_F
= U V^\top,\quad\text{where } \hat Z_{\text{tgt}}^\top \hat Z_{\text{src}} = U \Sigma V^\top,
\end{equation}
rotate the target into the source frame, and re-measure the accuracy of
$W_{\text{src}}$ on $\hat Z_{\text{tgt}} R^\star$. The fit uses only the geometric
concept correspondence (no human judgments), and being orthogonal it cannot rescale
or distort the geometry---so it neutralizes the arbitrary basis without performing
any of the alignment itself. If transfer stays weak after this control, weak
transfer reflects model-specific structure rather than independently-fit PCA axes;
we report $\rho_t$ both before (raw) and after (basis-aligned).

\paragraph{RSA.} We correlate (Spearman) the upper triangle of each model's
$1854\times1854$ cosine similarity matrix with that of the human SPoSE embedding,
before and after alignment.

\paragraph{Implementation.} Features are extracted once on a single GPU
(frozen, no backpropagation through the backbone). The aligner uses Adam
(learning rate $10^{-3}$, weight decay $\lambda{=}10^{-3}$), batch size 8192, up
to 30 epochs with early-stopping patience 4. The transfer experiment uses
shared dimensionality 256; robustness sweeps cap training at 1M triplets for
speed. Splits use a fixed seed; the full configuration is in
Appendix~\ref{app:impl}.

\section{Results}

\subsection{A cheap transform reaches \texorpdfstring{$\sim$}{~}90\% of the ceiling for all models}
Table~\ref{tab:main} reports zero-shot vs.\ aligned odd-one-out accuracy. Out of
the box, the contrastive models lead (CLIP $0.474$, SigLIP $0.468$), supervised
models follow (ViT $0.436$, ResNet $0.433$), and DINOv2 trails ($0.408$)---the
ordering reported in prior work. After a single linear transform, however, all
five converge to $0.59$--$0.62$, i.e.\ 88--92\% of the human ceiling. The
improvement is largest for the weakest baselines (DINOv2 $+0.203$, ResNet
$+0.179$) and smallest for the already-strong CLIP ($+0.130$), so the zero-shot
spread across training paradigms largely collapses into a narrow converged band
rather than leaving durable family-level differences. This near-ceiling,
architecture-insensitive alignability corroborates
\citet{muttenthaler2023human,muttenthaler2024aligning} rather than being a new
finding. Table~\ref{tab:main} reports mean$\pm$std over three seeds on full data
(per-model std $\le 0.003$), so the converged band is tight: the zero-shot family
spread ($0.066$) collapses to a residual $0.024$ that, while several times the seed
noise, is far smaller than the gap it replaces.

\subsection{The correction is largely shared across architectures}
\paragraph{Naive transfer looks model-specific.} Table~\ref{tab:transfer} reports the
raw transfer matrix in the shared 256-d space. Own-model transforms (diagonal) reach
$0.55$--$0.60$, but transferred transforms (off-diagonal) reach only $0.39$--$0.47$,
barely above the identity baseline ($0.35$--$0.39$): the average recovery fraction is
just $19.4\%$ (range $12.9\%$ for SigLIP to $26.9\%$ for ResNet), with CLIP the
strongest donor (mean transferred accuracy $0.423$). Taken at face value, this says the
learned correction is model-specific.

\paragraph{But it is almost entirely a PCA-basis artifact.} That conclusion does not
survive a basic control. Because each model's shared space is reduced
\emph{independently}, its principal axes are arbitrarily oriented, so $W_{\text{src}}$
applied to a target is largely penalized for basis mismatch rather than for genuine
representational difference. Applying the orthogonal-Procrustes alignment of
Section~\ref{sec:method} (\emph{Basis-alignment control})---a rigid, distortion-free
rotation fit on the shared concepts using no human labels---before transfer raises mean
recovery from $19.4\%$ to $\mathbf{88.1\%}$ (per model $84.4\%$--$91.6\%$;
Table~\ref{tab:recovery}, Fig.~\ref{fig:heatmap}). After this alignment, a transform
learned on \emph{any} backbone recovers most of \emph{any other} backbone's alignment
gain: off-diagonal accuracies ($0.52$--$0.59$) come within a few points of the
own-model diagonal ($0.55$--$0.60$). The human-alignment correction is therefore
largely \emph{shared} across self-supervised, contrastive, and supervised models, with
only a small residual ($\sim$12\%) that is genuinely architecture-specific.

\paragraph{The shared component is real, not an artifact of the rotation.} An orthogonal
map is distortion-free---it preserves all pairwise distances and angles---so it cannot
inject human-relevant structure the target lacks; it only re-orients existing structure,
and it is fit with no access to human judgments. Two synthetic checks confirm it behaves
correctly: when all models are noisy linear read-outs of a single \emph{shared} latent,
the procedure recovers that structure (raw $\sim$25\%, aligned $\sim$90\%); and
\emph{shuffling} the concept correspondence before fitting the rotation collapses the
gain back toward the raw level, showing the recovery comes from genuine cross-model
correspondence rather than the rotation's free parameters. Hence $88\%$ is, if anything,
a conservative (rigid-map) lower bound on how shared the correction is
(Fig.~\ref{fig:heatmap}).

\subsection{Representational similarity converges after alignment}
Raw Spearman RSA to the human SPoSE embedding varies widely
(Table~\ref{tab:rsa}): DINOv2 ($0.144$) and supervised ViT ($0.146$) are the most
``rotated away'' from human geometry, while CLIP ($0.419$) is closest. After
alignment, all models converge to $0.78$--$0.83$. That a linear map recovers
near-identical RSA from very different starting geometries reinforces
Finding~(i): the human-relevant structure is linearly accessible in all
backbones, even those whose untransformed geometry looks least human-like.

\subsection{A shared semantic ceiling}
Grouping residual errors by the THINGS 27 high-level categories (assigning each
test triplet the category of its odd-one-out item), the hardest categories
\emph{after} alignment are strikingly consistent across all five backbones:
\emph{office supply, furniture, kitchen tool, musical instrument, medical
equipment}, and \emph{toy} (Appendix~\ref{app:cats}). Concrete, perceptually
coherent categories (animals, food, fruit) are nearly solved, whereas abstract or
functional categories---whose membership depends on use or context more than
appearance---resist linear alignment regardless of architecture. The consistency
across backbones indicates this residual is a property of the human--model gap
itself, not of any single model, and marks a ceiling on what linear alignment can
achieve.

\subsection{Robustness}
Aligned accuracies are stable across random seeds (std $\le 0.003$ in
Table~\ref{tab:main}; Table~\ref{tab:robust}) and insensitive to the L2 strength over
$\{10^{-4},10^{-3},10^{-2}\}$ (variation $<0.02$, optimum at $10^{-3}$ for all
models). To quantify image-level leakage we compare a strict \emph{image-disjoint}
split (train/validation/test share no images, so $\sim$27\% of triplets---those
touching a held-out image---form the test set) against a \emph{size-matched random
triplet split} of the same pool, so the two differ only in whether train and test
share images. The leakage gap is small ($+0.013$ to $+0.056$): aligned accuracy
includes only a 1--6 point inflation from shared images, and even fully
image-disjoint, accuracy ($0.55$--$0.58$) far exceeds the zero-shot baselines
($0.41$--$0.47$). Notably, contrastive models leak least (CLIP $+0.013$, SigLIP
$+0.020$) while supervised/self-supervised models leak more (ViT $+0.032$, DINOv2
$+0.035$, ResNet $+0.056$), suggesting contrastive alignment is more
concept-general and less reliant on image-specific structure---echoing the
transfer result.

\section{Discussion}
Three observations cohere into a single picture. First, the \emph{level} of human
alignment achievable by a cheap linear map is essentially the same ($\sim$90\% of
ceiling) across self-supervised, contrastive, and supervised backbones: the
human-relevant similarity structure is linearly present in all of them, and
differences in zero-shot alignment mostly reflect how that structure is rotated
and scaled, not whether it exists. Second, the \emph{direction} of the required
correction is largely \emph{shared}: once the models are placed in a common frame by
a rigid, label-free rotation, a single transform transfers across architectures
($88\%$ recovery), up from an apparent $19\%$ when the arbitrary PCA bases are left
unaligned. There is thus an approximately universal linear ``human-alignment
direction,'' modulo each model's basis and a small architecture-specific
residual---not, as a naive transfer would suggest, a different patch for every model.
Third, what linear alignment cannot fix is shared: the
same abstract/functional categories remain hard everywhere, pointing to a
semantic component of the gap that is not a linear distortion at all.

\paragraph{Practical implications.} Because per-model linear alignment is cheap
(frozen features, a low-parameter map) and recovers most of the gap, it is an
attractive, low-risk way to make existing models more human-consistent without
retraining. And because the correction is largely shared, a transform learned once
can be re-used on a new backbone after only a cheap, label-free basis alignment---no
fresh human data required. The semantic ceiling, however, suggests that closing the
final $\sim$10\% will require non-linear maps, richer supervision, or changes to
pretraining---not a better linear probe.

\section{Limitations}
Our study uses a single CC0 reference image per concept, so it does not model
within-concept visual variation; behavioral (not neural) alignment; base-size
backbones only; and linear maps only. The image-disjoint analysis shows results
are not driven by image leakage, but the headline numbers follow the standard
triplet-level protocol and include a small (1--6 point) leakage component. The
transfer control uses an orthogonal (rigid) basis alignment by design---it is
distortion-free and cannot manufacture transferability---so the $88\%$ recovery is a
conservative lower bound on how shared the correction is; a richer affine or CCA map
could only raise it. Non-linear or hyperbolic
maps, larger model scales, and neural targets (THINGS-fMRI/MEG) are natural
extensions.

\section{Conclusion}
On THINGS odd-one-out, cheap linear transforms close most of the human--model
similarity gap uniformly across architectures, and---once models are placed in a
common coordinate frame---the \emph{same} correction transfers between them
($88\%$ recovery): the human-alignment direction is largely shared, not
model-specific as a naive transfer suggests. What remains is a shared semantic ceiling
on abstract/functional categories, indicating that the last $\sim$10\% of the gap is
non-linear and common to all models rather than a per-model linear distortion.

\section{Implementation details}\label{app:impl}
Features are extracted once from frozen backbones (no backpropagation through the
network) using each model's native preprocessing. Embeddings are standardized
per-dimension with training statistics. The aligner is a single matrix
$W\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times d}$ initialized to the identity, with a learnable
temperature on the cosine logits; it is trained with Adam (learning rate
$10^{-3}$), L2 weight decay $\lambda{=}10^{-3}$ on $W$ (none on the temperature),
batch size 8192, for up to 30 epochs with early-stopping patience 4 on validation
accuracy. For cross-model transfer, each model is standardized and PCA-reduced to
256 dimensions before learning $W$ in the shared space. Robustness runs cap
training at $10^{6}$ triplets for speed; seeds $\{0,1,2\}$ and
$\lambda\in\{10^{-4},10^{-3},10^{-2}\}$ are swept. The image-disjoint split holds
out 10\% of images each for validation and test; any triplet touching a held-out
image is assigned to the corresponding partition.
